1.1 - Classifying Matter

State of Matter & Kinetic-Molecular Theory

  • state - whether a substance is a solid, liquid, or gas.
  • kinetic-molecular theory - A theory of the behavior of matter at the molecular level.
    • In solids these particles are packed closely together, usually in a regular array. The particles vibrate back and forth about their average positions, but seldom does a particle in a solid squeeze past its immediate neighbors to come into contact with a new set of particles.
    • The atoms or molecules of liquids are arranged randomly rather than in the regular patterns found in solids. Liquids and gases are fluid because the particles are not confined to specific locations and can move past one another.
    • Under normal conditions, the particles in a gas are far apart. Gas molecules move extremely rapidly because they are not contained by their neighbors. The molecules of a gas fly about, colliding with one another and with the container walls. This random motion allows gas molecules to fill their container, so the volume of the gas sample is the volume of the container.
  • kinetic energy - the energy of a moving object, dependent on its mass and velocity.


Matter at the Macroscopic and Particulate Levels

  • macroscopic level - processes and properties on a scale large enough to be observed directly.
  • submicroscopic - representations of chemical phenomena in terms of atoms and molecules; also called particulate level.

Pure Substance

Every substance has a set of unique properties by which can be recognized.

A pure substance cannot be separated into two or more different species by any physical technique.

Mixtures: Homogeneous and Heterogeneous

  • mixture - a combination of two or more substances in which each substance retains its identity.
  • heterogeneous mixture - a mixture in which the properties in one region or sample are different from those in another region or sample.
  • homogeneous mixture - a mixture in which the properties are the same throughout, regardless of the optical resolution used to examine it.
  • solution - a homogeneous mixture.

1.2 - Elements and Atoms

  • elements - matter that is composed of only one kind of atom.

  • atom(s) - the smallest particle of an element that retains the characteristic chemical properties of that element.

1.3 - Compounds and Molecules

  • chemical bond - Any of several forces, especially the ionic bond, covalent bond, and metallic bond, by which atoms or ions are bound in a molecule or crystal.
  • compound - matter that is composed of two or more kinds of atoms chemically combined in definite proportions.
  • ions - an atom or group of atoms that has lost or gained one or more electrons s that it is no longer electrically neutral.
  • molecule - the smallest unit of a compound that retains the composition and properties of that compound.
  • chemical formula - a representation of a substance using symbols for its constituent elements.

  • chemical change - a change that involves the transformation of one or more substances into one or more different substances.
  • reactants - a starting substance in a chemical reaction.
  • product - a substance formed in chemical reaction.
  • chemical equation - a written representation of a chemical reaction, showing the reactants and products their physical states, and the direction in which the reaction proceeds.

1.6 - Units of Measurement

  • qualitative - non-numerical experimental observations, such as descriptive or comparative data.
  • quantitative - numerical experimental data, such as measurements of changes in mass or volume.

  • SI - abbreviation for Systeme International d'Unites, a uniform system of measurement units in which a single base unit is used for each measured physical quantity.

Volume

The SI unit of volume is the cubic meter (m3), which is far too large for everyday laboratory use. Therefore, chemists use the liter, symbolized by L.

1 liter(L) = 1000 mL = 1000 cm3

Example 1.3 - Units of Volume

Problem A laboratory beaker has a volume of 0.6 L. What is its volume in cubic centimeters, milliliters (mL), deciliters?

Solution
0.6 L x (100 cm3 / 1 L) = 600 cm3
600 cm3 = 600 mL
600 mL x (1 dL / 100 mL) = 6 dL

1.7 - Making Measurements: Precision, Accuracy, and Experimental Error

  • precision - the agreement of repeated measurements of a quantity with one another.
  • accuracy - the agreement between the measured quantity and the accepted value.
  • standard deviation - a measure of precision, calculated as the square root of the sum of the squares of the deviation for each measurement divided by the number of measurements.

Error = experimentally determined value - accepted value.

Percent error = (error in measurement / accepted value) * 100%

Example 1.4 - Precision and Accuracy

Problem A coin has an "accepted" diameter of 28.054 mm. In an experiment, two students measure this diameter. Student A makes 4 measurements of the diameter of a coin using a precision tool called a micrometer. Student B measures the same coin using a simple plastic ruler. The two students report the following results:

Student A (mm) Student B (mm)
28.246 27.9
28.244 28.0
28.246 27.8
28.248 28.1

What is the average diameter and percent error obtained in each case? Which student's data are more accurate? Which are more precise?

Solution

Student A (mm) Student B (mm)
28.246 27.9
28.244 28.0
28.246 27.8
28.248 28.1
Average = 28.246 Average = 28.0 mm

Student A's data are all very close to the average value, so they are quite precise. Student B's data, in contrast, have a wider range and are less precise. However, student A's result is less accurate that that of student B. The average diameter for student A differs from the "accepted" value by 0.192 mm and has a percent error of 0.684%:

Percent Error = [(28.246 mm - 28.054 mm) / 28.054 mm] X 100 = 0.684%

Student B's measurement has an error only about 0.2%

1.8 - Mathematics of Chemistry

Exponential or Scientific Notation

  • fixed notation - 505 m, 198 cm / yr
  • exponential or scientific notation (2.33 x 107 m) - a way of presenting very large or very small numbers in compact and consistent form that simplifies calculations.

In chemistry you will often have to use numbers in exponential notation in mathematical operations. The following five operations are important:

  • Adding and Subtracting Numbers Expressed in Scientific Notation

    • Convert them to the same powers of 10 and then add the digit terms.

  • Multiplication of Numbers Expressed in Scientific Notation

    • The digit terms are multiplied in the usual manner. The exponents are added algebraically.

  • Division of Numbers Expressed in Scientific Notation

    • The digit terms are multiplied in the usual manner. The exponents are subtracted algebraically.

  • Powers of Numbers Expressed in Scientific Notation

    • Treat digit terms are treated normally. Multiple the exponent by the power.

  • Roots of Numbers Expressed in Scientific Notation

    • Unless you have a calculator the number must first be put into a form in which the exponent is exactly divisible by the root.

Significant Figures

  • significant figures - the digits in a measured quantity that reflect the accuracy of the measurement.

In general, in a number representing a scientific measurement, the last digit to the right is taken to be inexact.

Rules used for significant figures

  • Rule 1. To determine the number of significant figures in a measurement, read the number from left to right and count all the digits, starting with the first digit that is not zero.

  • Rule 2. When adding or subtracting numbers, the number of decimal places in the answer is equal to the number of decimal places in the number with the fewest digits after the decimal.

  • Rule 3. In multiplication or division, the number of significant figures in the answer should be the same as that in the quantity with the fewest significant digits.

    • Rule 4. When a number is rounded off, the last digit to be retained is increased by one only if the following digit is 5 or greater.

  • dimensional analysis - a general problem-solving approach that uses the dimensions or units of each value to guide you through calculations.
  • conversion factor - a multiplier that relates the desired unto to the staring unit.

Number in original [new unit / original unit] = new number in new unit

Example 1.6 - Using Conversion Factors - Density in Different Units

Problem Oceanographers often express the density of sea water in units of kilograms per cubic meter. If the density of sea water is 1.025 g/cm3 at 15 oC what is its density in kilograms per cubic meter?

Solution

1.025 g (1 kg / 1000 g) = 1.025 x 10-3 kg

1 cm3 (1 m / 100 cm)3 = 1 x 10-6 m3

Density = 1.025 x 10-3 kg/ 1 x 10-6 m3 = 1.025 x 103 kg/m3

Example 1.7 - Problem Solving

Problem A mineral oil has a density of 0.875 g/cm3. Supposed you spread 0.75 g of this oil over the surface of water in a large dish with an inner diameter of 21.6 cm. How thick is the oil layer? Express the thickness in centimeters?

Strategy

Volume of oil layer = (thickness of layer) x (area of oil layer)

Need = volume of oil layer and the are of oil layer.

Solution

V = 0.75 g (1 cm3 / 0.875 g) = 0.86 cm3

A = Pi * (radius)2 = (3.1415)(10.8 cm)2 = 366 cm2

thickness = V/A = 0.0023 cm
